💡 Fun Facts

🐉

The building's roof is shaped like a dragon's back — the round colourful tiles are meant to be its scales. Legend has it the turret with the cross on top is St George's lance piercing the dragon.

🦴

Local nicknames for Casa Batlló include "Casa dels Ossos" (House of Bones) because the ground-floor balconies look like skulls and the pillars look like bones. Spooky or cool? Both.

🌊

Gaudí hated straight lines — he said there are none in nature. Every surface in the building curves, waves, or spirals. Even the doors and windows are irregular shapes.

💎

The facade is covered in broken ceramic tiles (trencadís), a technique Gaudí popularised. He sourced fragments from local ceramic factories — essentially making art from industrial waste.

🏗️

Originally built in 1877, Gaudí was commissioned by industrialist Josep Batlló in 1904 to renovate it. Neighbours thought he was mad. The result? A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

🧠 Trivia

🇪🇸 Barcelona Brain Teaser

Casa Batlló is one of how many Gaudí buildings on Passeig de Gràcia?

Two! Casa Batlló at number 43 and Casa Milà (La Pedrera) at number 92. They're both UNESCO sites and both absolutely bonkers. Gaudí really did turn this street into his personal gallery.

🤔 Quick Quiz

What does "Batlló" mean in Catalan?

It's actually a surname! Josep Batlló i Casanovas was the textile industrialist who commissioned Gaudí to redesign the building. The name doesn't have a particular meaning — it's just the family's name.
