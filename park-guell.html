💡 Fun Facts

🏘️

It was supposed to be a gated housing development — 60 luxury homes on a hill. Only two were ever built because nobody wanted to live in Gaudí's weird experimental neighbourhood. The project went bankrupt in 1914.

🦎

The famous salamander fountain ("El Drac") at the main entrance isn't actually a salamander — it's an alchemist's symbol. It's also the most photographed mosaic in Barcelona. Go early or it's selfie-stick chaos.

🪑

The serpentine bench on the main terrace is the longest mosaic bench in the world. Gaudí had a worker sit in wet plaster to get the ergonomics right. Uncomfortable art meets perfect posture.

🏔️

The main terrace offers panoramic views from the sea to the mountains. Gaudí positioned it as the "theatre" with Barcelona as the stage. On a clear day you can see Montserrat.

🎪

Gaudí lived in the park from 1906 to 1925 in the pink cottage (now a museum about him). He moved out after being arrested for refusing to speak Spanish — he answered police questions only in Catalan.

🧠 Trivia

🇪🇸 Park Quiz

Who originally commissioned Park Güell — and what was their business?

Eusebi Güell — a wealthy industrialist who made his fortune in textiles. He was Gaudí's biggest patron for decades. The park is named after him, not Gaudí. Without Güell's money, Gaudí would have been a very creative nobody.

🤔 Quick Quiz

How many of the planned 60 houses were actually built in Park Güell?

Two. The show model home (designed by Gaudí's assistant) and Gaudí's own pink cottage. That's a 97% failure rate on real estate. Barcelona's worst housing development — and its best park.
