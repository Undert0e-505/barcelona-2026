🗺️ Walking Route

Follow the stops in order. Each stop matches a waypoint (A–F) in Google Maps. Tap "Open Route in Maps" to get turn-by-turn walking navigation.

A

Torre Glòries

Jean Nouvel's bullet-shaped tower with 4,500 coloured glass panels. Start here — it's the unmistakable landmark that marks the edge of the 22@ district.

Plaça de les Glòries · Metro L1 Glòries
B

Parc del Centre del Poblenou

Walk 2 minutes south to this Jean Nouvel-designed park. The perimeter walls are covered in Mediterranean plants and the building facades around the park feature murals — look for Escif's Classical Column, one of the first serious urban art pieces in the neighbourhood.

Carrer del Marroc · 2 min from start
C

Carrer de Pere IV + La Escocesa

Head southeast along Pere IV — this is the street art backbone of Poblenou. The factory facades are the neighbourhood's most active canvas. Murals change constantly: Axel Void, Miss Van, Sixe Paredes, and Roc Blackblock have all painted here. At number 345 you'll find La Escocesa — a 19th-century Scottish textile factory turned artist studio centre with 21 active studios. The exterior walls carry murals by Miss Van and Zosen Bandido.

Carrer de Pere IV · La Escocesa at #345 · ~30 min walk with stops
D

Borondo's "Fer Llenya" mural

At the corner of Lope de Vega and Pallars — one of the largest and most impressive murals in Poblenou. Castellers (human towers) in expressionist brushwork with dripping colours. Painted during the 2015 Open Walls Conference and still standing, which is rare for this neighbourhood.

Carrer de Pallars & Carrer de Lope de Vega
E

Selva de Mar & Carrer del Perú

Turn left onto Selva de Mar — the block between here and Carrer del Perú is one of the densest graffiti zones in Barcelona. Walls painted and repainted constantly by local artists including Rubicon1. Continue east to Carrer del Perú for more wall-to-wall colour, then double back west along Selva de Mar to spot murals on the buildings you passed.

Carrer de la Selva de Mar & Carrer del Perú · ~15 min
F

Rambla del Poblenou

Finish on the neighbourhood's main boulevard. Grab a coffee or a vermouth at one of the terraces — you've earned it. The Rambla runs all the way down to the beach if you want to continue to Bogatell or Mar Bella.

End of route · cafés and terraces

💡 Fun Facts

🏭

Poblenou was Spain's largest industrial district in the 19th century — textile factories, chemical plants, and warehouses. When the factories closed in the 60s and 70s, artists moved into the empty buildings and started painting the walls.

🔄

The murals are deliberately temporary. Most are on facades of buildings awaiting demolition or renovation, so they change every few months. Every visit is a different route. You're basically walking through a gallery that rehangs itself.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁳󠁣󠁴󠁿

La Escocesa means "The Scottish One" — it was built by Scottish textile investors in the 19th century. Now it's a 21-studio artist centre run entirely by the artists who work in it. The only model of its kind in Poblenou.

🏢

In 2000, Barcelona City Council launched Project 22@ to transform the old industrial neighbourhood into a tech district. The result is a surreal mix: cutting-edge glass offices next to graffiti-covered ruins next to hipster coffee shops.

🎨

The Open Walls Conference has brought international artists to paint legally on Poblenou's walls for years — including Axel Void, Miss Van, Sam3, and Borondo. The "Fer Llenya" mural by Borondo (stop D) has survived since 2015, which is basically eternity in street art years.

🧠 Trivia

🎨 Street Art Quiz

What does "Poblenou" literally mean in Catalan?

"New Village" — from "poble" (village/people) and "nou" (new). It got the name in the 19th century when the area was first developed. These days it's new again — just in a very different way.

🤔 Quick Quiz

Why do the murals on Carrer de Pere IV change so often?

Because most of the walls belong to buildings awaiting demolition or renovation. The Wallspot system coordinates legal mural rotation — artists apply for wall space through an official programme. When a building gets redeveloped, the wall (and the mural) disappears. New wall, new art.

📖 Source & Further Reading

This route is based on the excellent Poblenou Creative District Walking Route by Barcelona Urbana — a detailed guide covering the history, the artists, and the 22@ transformation. Highly recommended reading before you go.

Key highlights from their guide:

  • Poblenou was Spain's largest industrial district in the 19th century — the same blocks that held the factories now contain over 12,000 tech and creative companies
  • In 2000, Barcelona City Council launched Project 22@ to transform the old industrial neighbourhood into the city's Silicon Valley
  • The Wallspot system coordinates legal mural rotation — artists apply for wall space through an official programme, which is why the art changes so frequently
  • The Open Walls Conference has brought international artists including Axel Void, Miss Van, Sixe Paredes, Sam3, and Borondo to paint legally on Poblenou's walls
  • La Escocesa has Fàbrica de Creació certification from Barcelona City Council — the only model in Poblenou managed entirely by the artists who work in it
  • The Disseny Hub Barcelona (Plaça de les Glòries) is free on Sundays from 15:00, and its terraces have a direct visual axis to Torre Glòries
  • First weekend of the month: Palo Alto Market in the former Ramón Gal factory — design and craft market in an industrial setting
